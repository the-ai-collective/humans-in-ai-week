# Thursday: Dinner — Best Practices

---

## What makes a great Dinner event

Curated seating. Mix industries, backgrounds, and AI experience levels at each table. The food creates a shared ritual — breaking bread lowers defenses. Prompt cards keep conversation structured without being rigid. The best dinners feel like the kind of conversation you wish happened more often.

---

## Catering logistics

- Confirm dietary restrictions via RSVP form (add a field — see below)
- Family-style serving encourages sharing and builds connection
- Budget **$25–60 per person** depending on city
- Alcohol is optional — always provide non-alcoholic alternatives
- Coordinate course timing with discussion rounds: serve between rounds, not during
- Have the first course already on tables or plated before the HQ Opening

---

## Dietary restrictions form

Add to your Luma RSVP: *"Any dietary restrictions? (vegetarian, vegan, gluten-free, halal, kosher, allergies)"*

Share the compiled list with your caterer **72 hours before** the event.

---

## Table assignment strategy

Mix deliberately. Don't let friends sit together.

- Pair: tech + non-tech, senior + junior, different industries, different genders
- If you know attendees (from RSVP data), curate tables manually
- If you don't, randomize but ensure visible diversity at each table
- Cap tables at **6–8 people**. At 10, side conversations split the group.

---

## Prompt card design

Print on cardstock. One question per card, large readable font.

**Structure:**
- Front: The discussion question
- Back: A "conversation starter" follow-up to break the ice

**Example:**
- Front: *"If AI could do 50% of your job tomorrow, which 50% would you keep?"*
- Back: *"Start by sharing what surprised you about your own answer."*

---

## Common mistakes

- **Tables too large** — Max 8. At 10, the table splits into two conversations and you lose the structure.
- **No prompt cards** — Without them, tables default to small talk or one person monologuing.
- **Poor course timing** — Food arriving during report-backs is distracting. Coordinate with caterer.
- **Not assigning table facilitators** — At least one person per table should know their job is to keep the conversation on track.
- **Forgetting to collect insights** — Each table needs a note-taker. Report-backs without notes are vague.

---

## Scaling guidance

| Size | Adjustments |
|------|------------|
| 20–40 | 3–5 tables, intimate feel. Host can visit each table during rounds |
| 40–80 | 5–10 tables, assign table facilitators. Report-backs are essential |
| 80+ | Add a brief keynote or panel (15 min max) between Rounds 2 and 3 to reset energy |

---

## Budget note

This is the **highest-cost format**. Typical range: **$500–$3,000** depending on city, venue, and headcount. Sponsor coverage is essential. Position it as an "exclusive dinner" in your sponsor pitch — high visibility, intimate access to the audience.
