# Tuesday: Research Roundtable — Best Practices

---

## What makes a great Research Roundtable

Diverse disciplines. At least one panelist from a non-technical field — philosophy, law, sociology, medicine. The moderator's job is to be the audience's translator: when jargon appears, interrupt and translate. The best roundtables feel like eavesdropping on a conversation between brilliant people who disagree respectfully.

---

## Panelist briefing

Share discussion questions **1 week before** the event. Ask each panelist to prepare ONE concrete finding or example — no slide decks, no prepared statements. Set expectations clearly:

- Accessible language. No jargon without explanation.
- This is a conversation, not a series of lectures.
- Brief them on audience composition (technical vs. general, age range, industries).
- Confirm: 10-min intros, 45-min moderated discussion, 20-min audience Q&A.

---

## Jargon management

The moderator should interrupt politely when jargon appears: "Can you explain what you mean by [term] for everyone?" This is a feature, not a bug — it makes the conversation better for the whole room and often surfaces the most interesting explanations.

---

## Common mistakes

- **All panelists from the same field** — Mix CS researchers with ethicists, social scientists, industry practitioners
- **Letting one voice dominate** — Moderator must redirect: "Great point. [Other panelist], how does that land from your perspective?"
- **Questions that are too broad** — "Tell us about AI" gets a lecture. "What's the one finding from your lab this year that scared you?" gets a conversation.
- **No audience interaction until Q&A** — Sprinkle in 1–2 audience questions during the moderated section to keep energy up
- **Reading prepared statements** — If a panelist pulls out notes, gently redirect to conversation mode

---

## Scaling guidance

| Size | Adjustments |
|------|------------|
| Under 40 | Roundtable format: chairs in a circle, panelists as equals with audience |
| 40–100 | Standard panel format with clear stage/audience separation |
| 100+ | Add a post-panel breakout session where attendees cluster around individual panelists |
